% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Behavior-aware state estimation promises safer autonomous navigation by predicting pedestrian intent, yet no systematic validation methodology exists for these perception-level components.
We present \textsc{Simplex-Track}, a comprehensive validation framework for a Social Force Extended Kalman Filter (SF-EKF) pedestrian tracker deployed in the CARLA simulator with YOLOv9 detection and DeepSORT association.
Following the validation pipeline of Kochenderfer et al.\ \cite{kochenderfer2025validation}, we: (1)~formalize the tracking system as a stochastic process and specify safety properties in Signal Temporal Logic; (2)~apply direct Monte Carlo simulation and optimization-based falsification to discover failure modes; (3)~characterize the failure distribution via Metropolis--Hastings MCMC; (4)~estimate failure probabilities using importance sampling and the cross-entropy method, achieving a 5.2$\times$ variance reduction over direct sampling; and (5)~deploy a Simplex runtime monitor with TTC-based switching that reduces the collision rate from 7.8\% to 2.1\% at medium pedestrian density.
Bayesian estimation with a $\mathrm{Beta}$ posterior yields a 95\% credible interval of $[0.057, 0.101]$ for the failure probability under nominal conditions.
Our analysis demonstrates that the SF-EKF reduces collisions by 52\% over a constant-velocity baseline, with remaining failures concentrated in high-density, close-range, broadside-approach scenarios identified through MCMC sampling.
\end{abstract}

Safety validation, pedestrian tracking, Simplex architecture, importance sampling, signal temporal logic, extended Kalman filter, social force model
\end{IEEEkeywords}

% I. INTRODUCTION
% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Autonomous vehicles operating in urban environments must accurately track pedestrians to avoid collisions, yet the perception-level state estimators that underpin tracking decisions have received remarkably little formal validation attention.
Standard constant-velocity (CV) Kalman filters~\cite{barshalom2001estimation} assume linear motion---an assumption routinely violated by pedestrians who swerve, stop abruptly, or respond to social cues from nearby agents.
Behavior-aware filters that incorporate pedestrian interaction models, such as the Social Force Model (SFM)~\cite{helbing1995social}, promise more accurate predictions but introduce complex nonlinear dynamics whose failure modes are poorly understood.

This gap matters because tracking errors propagate directly into safety-critical decisions.
An overestimated Time-To-Collision (TTC)~\cite{minderhoud2001ttc} can delay emergency braking; an underestimated TTC can trigger nuisance stops that degrade traffic flow and erode user trust.
Despite extensive work on trajectory prediction benchmarks~\cite{pellegrini2009youll, alahi2016social}, the field lacks a systematic methodology for \emph{validating} tracker safety---quantifying failure probability, characterizing failure distributions, and providing runtime guarantees.

We address this gap by applying the full validation pipeline from \emph{Algorithms for Validation}~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation} to a behavior-aware pedestrian tracking system.
Our system, \textsc{Simplex-Track}, integrates:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{perception pipeline} (YOLOv9~\cite{wang2024yolov9} $\to$ DeepSORT~\cite{wojke2017deepsort} $\to$ Social Force EKF) as the advanced channel;
    \item A \textbf{TTC-based Simplex monitor}~\cite{sha2001simplex, bak2014simplex} as the runtime safety layer;
    \item A \textbf{deterministic emergency braking controller} as the verified baseline.
\end{enumerate}

Our contributions map directly to the textbook's validation framework:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{System Model} (Ch.~2): Formal stochastic system definition with CT-model EKF dynamics and social force interactions.
    \item \textbf{Safety Specification} (Ch.~3): STL safety property with quantitative robustness metrics.
    \item \textbf{Falsification} (Ch.~4--5): Optimization-based search via CMA-ES for worst-case pedestrian configurations.
    \item \textbf{Failure Distribution} (Ch.~6): MCMC characterization of failure-inducing parameter regions.
    \item \textbf{Failure Probability} (Ch.~7): Importance sampling and cross-entropy estimation with Bayesian credible intervals.
    \item \textbf{Runtime Monitoring} (Ch.~12): ODD monitoring, uncertainty quantification, and Simplex architecture.
\end{enumerate}

% II. SYSTEM MODEL
% ============================================================
\section{System Model}
\label{sec:system}

Following Kochenderfer et al.~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}, Ch.~2, we formalize the tracking system as a discrete-time stochastic process.

\subsection{State and Observation Spaces}

The system state at time $k$ comprises the ego vehicle state $\mathbf{s}_k^{\text{ego}}$ and $N_k$ tracked pedestrian states:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}_k = \bigl(\mathbf{s}_k^{\text{ego}},\; \{\mathbf{s}_k^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^{N_k}\bigr)
\end{equation}
Each pedestrian state in the EKF is a 5-dimensional vector under the Coordinated Turn (CT) motion model~\cite{li2003survey}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{s}^{(i)}_k = \begin{bmatrix} p_x & p_y & v & \phi & \omega \end{bmatrix}^\top
    \label{eq:ct_state}
\end{equation}
where $(p_x, p_y)$ is position, $v$ is speed, $\phi$ is heading, and $\omega$ is turn rate.

The observation at each step is a set of bounding-box detections from YOLOv9, associated to tracks by DeepSORT:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{z}_k^{(i)} = \begin{bmatrix} p_x & p_y \end{bmatrix}^\top + \mathbf{w}_k, \quad \mathbf{w}_k \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{R})
\end{equation}
with measurement noise $\mathbf{R} = \mathrm{diag}(0.2^2, 0.2^2)$.

\subsection{Coordinated Turn Dynamics}

The CT model propagates the state forward by $\Delta t$:
\begin{equation}
    f(\mathbf{s}_k) = \begin{cases}
    \begin{bmatrix}
        p_x + \frac{v}{\omega}\bigl(\sin(\phi + \omega\Delta t) - \sin\phi\bigr) \\
        p_y + \frac{v}{\omega}\bigl(\cos\phi - \cos(\phi + \omega\Delta t)\bigr) \\
        v \\
        \phi + \omega\Delta t \\
        \omega
    \end{bmatrix} & |\omega| \geq \epsilon \\[12pt]
    \begin{bmatrix}
        p_x + v\cos\phi\;\Delta t \\
        p_y + v\sin\phi\;\Delta t \\
        v \\
        \phi \\
        \omega
    \end{bmatrix} & |\omega| < \epsilon
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:ct_dynamics}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon = 10^{-4}$ guards against the singularity at zero turn rate.

\subsection{Social Force Model}

Following Helbing and Moln\'{a}r~\cite{helbing1995social}, we augment the motion model with a repulsive social force between pedestrians.
For pedestrian $i$ with neighbors $\{j\}$, the social force is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{f}_{\text{soc}}^{(i)} = \sum_{j \neq i} A_{\text{soc}} \exp\!\left(\frac{2r_{\text{ped}} - d_{ij}}{B_{\text{soc}}}\right) \hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ij}
    \label{eq:social_force}
\end{equation}
where $d_{ij} = \|\mathbf{p}_i - \mathbf{p}_j\|_2$ is the inter-pedestrian distance, $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ij}$ is the unit vector from $j$ to $i$, and the parameters are $A_{\text{soc}} = 2.0$\,N, $B_{\text{soc}} = 0.5$\,m, $r_{\text{ped}} = 0.3$\,m.
The force only activates for neighbors within a 3\,m radius.

\subsection{EKF Prediction and Update}

The prediction step computes the Jacobian $\mathbf{F}_k$ of the CT model~\eqref{eq:ct_dynamics} and propagates the covariance:
\begin{align}
    \hat{\mathbf{s}}_{k|k-1} &= f(\mathbf{s}_{k-1}) \label{eq:ekf_predict_mean} \\
    \mathbf{P}_{k|k-1} &= \mathbf{F}_k \mathbf{P}_{k-1} \mathbf{F}_k^\top + \mathbf{Q} \label{eq:ekf_predict_cov}
\end{align}
with process noise $\mathbf{Q} = \mathrm{diag}(1.0^2, 1.0^2, 1.9^2, 3.0^2, 3.0^2)$.
The update step uses the measurement matrix $\mathbf{H} = [\mathbf{I}_2 \mid \mathbf{0}_{2\times3}]$:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{K}_k &= \mathbf{P}_{k|k-1}\mathbf{H}^\top(\mathbf{H}\mathbf{P}_{k|k-1}\mathbf{H}^\top + \mathbf{R})^{-1} \\
    \mathbf{s}_{k|k} &= \hat{\mathbf{s}}_{k|k-1} + \mathbf{K}_k(\mathbf{z}_k - \mathbf{H}\hat{\mathbf{s}}_{k|k-1}) \\
    \mathbf{P}_{k|k} &= (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K}_k\mathbf{H})\mathbf{P}_{k|k-1}
\end{align}

The BehavioralEKFFilter wraps this 5D core in an 8D state $[x, y, a, h, v_x, v_y, v_a, v_h]$ for DeepSORT compatibility, with Jacobian-based coordinate transformations between the $(v, \phi)$ and $(v_x, v_y)$ representations.

\subsection{Disturbance Model}

The scenario disturbance $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ parameterizes the stochastic environment:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\tau} = (N_{\text{ped}}, \{d_i^{\text{spawn}}, \theta_i^{\text{approach}}, v_i^{\text{init}}\}_{i=1}^{N_{\text{ped}}})
    \label{eq:disturbance}
\end{equation}
where $N_{\text{ped}} \in \{3,5,7,10\}$ is pedestrian count, $d_i^{\text{spawn}} \sim \mathcal{U}[5, 35]$\,m is spawn distance, $\theta_i^{\text{approach}} \sim \mathcal{U}[0, \pi]$ is approach angle relative to the ego heading, and $v_i^{\text{init}} \sim \mathcal{U}[0.5, 2.0]$\,m/s is initial speed.
Under the nominal distribution $p(\boldsymbol{\tau})$, pedestrians are uniformly distributed across the spawn region.

% ============================================================
% III. SAFETY PROPERTY SPECIFICATION
% ============================================================
\section{Safety Property Specification}
\label{sec:stl}

Following Kochenderfer et al.~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}, Ch.~3, we specify safety properties using Signal Temporal Logic (STL)~\cite{maler2004stl} with quantitative robustness semantics~\cite{donze2010robust}.

\subsection{Time-To-Collision}

For each tracked pedestrian $i$, we compute TTC from the estimated states:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{TTC}_k^{(i)} = \frac{d_k^{(i)} - d_{\min}}{v_{\text{rel},k}^{(i)}} \quad \text{if } v_{\text{rel},k}^{(i)} > 0
    \label{eq:ttc}
\end{equation}
where $d_k^{(i)} = \|\hat{\mathbf{p}}_k^{(i)} - \mathbf{p}_k^{\text{ego}}\|_2$ and $v_{\text{rel},k}^{(i)}$ is the closing velocity along the line-of-sight.
When $v_{\text{rel}} \leq 0$ (diverging), we set $\mathrm{TTC} = \infty$.

\subsection{STL Safety Specification}

We define the primary safety property:
\begin{equation}
    \varphi_{\text{safe}} = \Box_{[0,T]}\!\left(\bigwedge_{i=1}^{N_{\text{ped}}} \bigl(\mathrm{TTC}_k^{(i)} \geq \tau_{\text{safe}} \;\lor\; a_{\text{brake}} = \text{active}\bigr)\right)
    \label{eq:stl_spec}
\end{equation}
Reading: ``\emph{Always}, for all pedestrians, either TTC exceeds the safety threshold $\tau_{\text{safe}}$, or emergency braking is active.''

\subsection{Quantitative Robustness}

The STL robustness metric~\cite{donze2010robust} maps a signal $\xi$ and specification $\varphi$ to $\rho(\varphi, \xi, k) \in \mathbb{R}$:
\begin{align}
    \rho(\mathrm{TTC}^{(i)} \geq \tau, \xi, k) &= \mathrm{TTC}_k^{(i)} - \tau_{\text{safe}} \label{eq:robustness_atom} \\
    \rho(\Box_{[0,T]} \varphi, \xi, 0) &= \min_{k \in [0,T]} \rho(\varphi, \xi, k) \label{eq:robustness_always}
\end{align}
Positive robustness $\rho > 0$ indicates satisfaction with margin; $\rho < 0$ indicates violation with severity proportional to $|\rho|$.
We use $\rho_{\min} = \min_i \rho(\varphi_{\text{safe}}, \xi, 0)$ as the aggregate safety metric.

\subsection{Composite Performance Metric}

Beyond collision avoidance, we evaluate tracking accuracy via Average Displacement Error (ADE) and Final Displacement Error (FDE):
\begin{align}
    \text{ADE}^{(i)} &= \frac{1}{T}\sum_{k=1}^{T}\|\hat{\mathbf{p}}_k^{(i)} - \mathbf{p}_k^{(i),\text{gt}}\|_2 \\
    \text{FDE}^{(i)} &= \|\hat{\mathbf{p}}_T^{(i)} - \mathbf{p}_T^{(i),\text{gt}}\|_2
\end{align}
These complement the safety metric by quantifying prediction quality.

% IV. FALSIFICATION AND FAILURE ANALYSIS
% ============================================================
\section{Falsification and Failure Analysis}
\label{sec:falsification}

We follow the falsification methodology from Kochenderfer et al.~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}, Ch.~4--6 and Corso et al.~\cite{corso2021survey}, applying increasingly sophisticated methods to discover and characterize failures.

\subsection{Direct Monte Carlo Simulation}
\label{sec:direct_mc}

We first establish a baseline failure rate through direct sampling.
Drawing $N = 500$ scenarios from the nominal distribution $p(\boldsymbol{\tau})$, we define a failure event as:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau}) = \mathbb{1}\!\bigl[\exists\, k, i : \mathrm{TTC}_k^{(i)} \leq 0 \;\text{(collision)}\bigr]
    \label{eq:failure_event}
\end{equation}
The maximum likelihood estimate of the failure probability is:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{p}_{\text{fail}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N} \mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau}_n) = \frac{n_{\text{fail}}}{N}
    \label{eq:mle_pfail}
\end{equation}
with a 95\% confidence interval via the normal approximation:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{p}_{\text{fail}} \pm z_{0.975}\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_{\text{fail}}(1-\hat{p}_{\text{fail}})}{N}}
\end{equation}

At $N_{\text{ped}} = 5$ (medium density), we observe $n_{\text{fail}} = 39$ failures in $500$ trials, yielding $\hat{p}_{\text{fail}} = 0.078$ with 95\% CI $[0.054, 0.102]$ for the SF-EKF.
The CV baseline exhibits $\hat{p}_{\text{fail}}^{\text{CV}} = 0.164$, confirming that the social force model provides substantial improvement.

\subsection{Optimization-Based Falsification}
\label{sec:falsification_opt}

Following Ch.~4 of~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}, we formulate falsification as an optimization problem over the disturbance space:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\tau}^* = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\tau}} \;\rho_{\min}(\boldsymbol{\tau})
    \label{eq:falsification_opt}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_{\min}$ is the minimum STL robustness across all pedestrians and time steps.
We parameterize $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ as in~\eqref{eq:disturbance} and solve using CMA-ES~\cite{hansen2003cmaes} with population size $\lambda = 20$ and $\sigma_0 = 0.3$.

CMA-ES converges within 85 evaluations to a worst-case scenario with $\rho_{\min} = -3.2$\,s, corresponding to a collision with a pedestrian spawning at $d^{\text{spawn}} = 8.5$\,m, $\theta^{\text{approach}} = 82^\circ$ (near-broadside), and $v^{\text{init}} = 1.8$\,m/s.
This identifies the \emph{critical failure mode}: close-range pedestrians approaching from near-perpendicular angles, where the social force has minimal effect (no prior heading information) and the CT model's turn-rate estimate lags behind abrupt trajectory changes.

\subsection{Failure Distribution Characterization via MCMC}
\label{sec:mcmc}

To characterize the \emph{distribution} of failure-inducing scenarios, we apply Metropolis--Hastings MCMC~\cite{metropolis1953equation, hastings1970monte} following Ch.~6 of~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}.

The target distribution is the conditional failure distribution:
\begin{equation}
    p_{\text{fail}}(\boldsymbol{\tau}) \propto p(\boldsymbol{\tau}) \cdot \mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau})
    \label{eq:failure_dist}
\end{equation}
We use a Gaussian proposal kernel $q(\boldsymbol{\tau}' \mid \boldsymbol{\tau}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\tau}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_q)$ with $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_q = \mathrm{diag}(2.0^2, 10.0^2, 0.2^2)$ for $(d^{\text{spawn}}, \theta^{\text{approach}}, v^{\text{init}})$.

The acceptance ratio is:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha(\boldsymbol{\tau}' \mid \boldsymbol{\tau}) = \min\!\left(1, \frac{p(\boldsymbol{\tau}')\,\mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau}')}{p(\boldsymbol{\tau})\,\mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau})} \cdot \frac{q(\boldsymbol{\tau} \mid \boldsymbol{\tau}')}{q(\boldsymbol{\tau}' \mid \boldsymbol{\tau})}\right)
\end{equation}
Since $p$ is uniform and $q$ is symmetric, this simplifies to accepting proposals that produce failures and rejecting those that do not.

We run 2000 MCMC steps with a burn-in of 200, yielding 1800 accepted failure samples.
Fig.~\ref{fig:failure_dist} visualizes the resulting distribution in $(d^{\text{spawn}}, \theta^{\text{approach}})$ space.
The failure density concentrates in the region $d^{\text{spawn}} \in [5, 14]$\,m and $\theta^{\text{approach}} \in [55^\circ, 110^\circ]$, confirming the critical failure mode identified by CMA-ES and revealing that it represents a \emph{region} rather than a single worst-case point.

% V. FAILURE PROBABILITY ESTIMATION
% ============================================================
\section{Failure Probability Estimation}
\label{sec:failure_prob}

The rare-event nature of collisions ($p_{\text{fail}} \approx 0.08$ under nominal conditions) makes direct Monte Carlo inefficient for precise estimation.
Following Ch.~7 of~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}, we apply importance sampling and the cross-entropy method to reduce variance.

\subsection{Direct Bayesian Estimation}
\label{sec:bayesian}

We first apply a Bayesian approach with a conjugate Beta prior~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}:
\begin{equation}
    p_{\text{fail}} \sim \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_0, \beta_0)
\end{equation}
Using a weakly informative prior $\mathrm{Beta}(5, 60)$ (encoding a prior belief of $\approx$8\% failure rate with moderate uncertainty), the posterior after observing $n = 39$ failures in $m = 500$ trials is:
\begin{equation}
    p_{\text{fail}} \mid \mathcal{D} \sim \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_0 + n, \; \beta_0 + m - n) = \mathrm{Beta}(44, 521)
    \label{eq:beta_posterior}
\end{equation}
with MAP estimate $\hat{p}_{\text{MAP}} = \frac{\alpha - 1}{\alpha + \beta - 2} = 0.076$ and 95\% credible interval $[0.057, 0.101]$.
Fig.~\ref{fig:bayesian} shows the posterior distribution.

\begin{figure}[!t]
    \centering
    \input{figures/bayesian_posterior.tex}
    \caption{Bayesian posterior $\mathrm{Beta}(44, 521)$ on $p_{\text{fail}}$ with 95\% credible interval (red bracket). The prior $\mathrm{Beta}(5, 60)$ (dashed mauve) is updated by $39$ observed failures in $500$ trials, following the Bayesian estimation framework of~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}, Ch.~7.}
    \label{fig:bayesian}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Importance Sampling}
\label{sec:importance_sampling}

Following \cite{kochenderfer2025validation}, Ch.~7.2, we construct a proposal distribution $q(\boldsymbol{\tau})$ that over-samples the failure region identified by MCMC (Section~\ref{sec:mcmc}):
\begin{equation}
    q(\boldsymbol{\tau}) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_q, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_q\right), \quad \boldsymbol{\mu}_q = \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 85 \\ 1.5 \end{bmatrix}, \; \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_q = \mathrm{diag}(4^2, 20^2, 0.3^2)
\end{equation}
centered on the identified failure region.
The IS estimator is:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{p}_{\text{fail}}^{\text{IS}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N} \mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau}_n) \cdot w(\boldsymbol{\tau}_n), \quad w(\boldsymbol{\tau}_n) = \frac{p(\boldsymbol{\tau}_n)}{q(\boldsymbol{\tau}_n)}
    \label{eq:is_estimator}
\end{equation}
The effective sample size (ESS) is:
\begin{equation}
    \text{ESS} = \frac{\left(\sum_n w_n\right)^2}{\sum_n w_n^2}
\end{equation}

With $N = 500$ IS samples, we obtain $\hat{p}_{\text{fail}}^{\text{IS}} = 0.076$ with standard error $0.005$, compared to the direct MC standard error of $0.012$.
This represents a variance reduction factor of $5.2\times$.

\subsection{Cross-Entropy Method}
\label{sec:cross_entropy}

Following~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}, Ch.~7.3 and Rubinstein~\cite{rubinstein1999crossentropy, rubinstein2004crossentropy}, we iteratively adapt the proposal distribution to minimize the KL divergence to the optimal IS distribution.

At each iteration $\ell$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Draw $N = 200$ samples from $q_\ell(\boldsymbol{\tau})$.
    \item Evaluate $\mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau}_n)$ for each sample.
    \item Select the top $\rho$-quantile ($\rho = 0.1$) of samples by robustness $\rho_{\min}$.
    \item Update $q_{\ell+1}$ by maximum likelihood on the elite samples.
\end{enumerate}
After 8 iterations, the CE method converges to a proposal with $\hat{p}_{\text{fail}}^{\text{CE}} = 0.077$, consistent with the IS and direct estimates.
Fig.~\ref{fig:is_convergence} compares convergence across all three methods.

\begin{figure}[!t]
    \centering
    \input{figures/importance_sampling.tex}
    \caption{Convergence of $\hat{p}_{\text{fail}}$ vs.\ number of samples for Direct MC, Importance Sampling, and Cross-Entropy methods. IS and CE converge within $\sim$400 samples, while Direct MC requires $>3000$. Following~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}, Ch.~7.}
    \label{fig:is_convergence}
\end{figure}

% VI. RUNTIME MONITORING
% ============================================================
\section{Runtime Monitoring with Simplex Architecture}
\label{sec:runtime}

The validation analysis above characterizes system-level failure behavior offline.
As an additional safety layer, we implement runtime monitoring following Ch.~12 of~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}, using the Simplex Architecture~\cite{sha2001simplex, bak2014simplex}.

\subsection{Operational Design Domain Monitoring}
\label{sec:odd}

Following Ch.~12.1 of~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}, we define the Operational Design Domain (ODD) as the set of conditions under which the SF-EKF is validated:
\begin{equation}
    \text{ODD} = \{N_{\text{ped}} \leq N_{\max},\; v_{\text{ego}} \in [0, v_{\max}],\; \text{visibility} \geq V_{\min}\}
\end{equation}
with $N_{\max} = 10$, $v_{\max} = 30$\,km/h, $V_{\min} = 50$\,m.
We monitor ODD compliance using a $k$-NN classifier on tracking state features (number of active tracks, mean inter-pedestrian distance, ego speed).
An ODD violation triggers a conservative speed reduction.

\subsection{Uncertainty Quantification}
\label{sec:uq}

Following Ch.~12.2 of~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}, we leverage the EKF covariance $\mathbf{P}_{k|k}$ as a real-time uncertainty measure:
\begin{equation}
    u_k^{(i)} = \mathrm{tr}\!\left(\mathbf{P}_{k|k}^{(i)}[0:2, 0:2]\right)
    \label{eq:uncertainty}
\end{equation}
When $u_k^{(i)} > u_{\max}$ for any tracked pedestrian, the monitor increases $\tau_{\text{safe}}$ by a safety margin $\Delta\tau = 0.5$\,s to compensate for state estimation uncertainty.

\subsection{Simplex Architecture Design}
\label{sec:simplex}

The complete Simplex monitor (Fig.~\ref{fig:system_arch}) comprises three components:

\paragraph{Advanced Controller (AC)}
The SF-EKF perception pipeline provides state estimates $\hat{\mathbf{s}}_k^{(i)}$ used by the ego planner for nominal navigation.

\paragraph{Decision Module (DM)}
The TTC-based monitor evaluates the STL specification~\eqref{eq:stl_spec} at every time step.
The switching condition is:
\begin{equation}
    \text{switch}(k) = \exists\, i : \mathrm{TTC}_k^{(i)} < \tau_{\text{safe}} \;\land\; a_{\text{brake}} \neq \text{active}
    \label{eq:switch}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Baseline Controller (BC)}
Upon triggering, the baseline applies maximum deceleration:
\begin{equation}
    a_{\text{brake}} = -a_{\max}, \quad d_{\text{stop}} = \frac{v_k^2}{2a_{\max}}
    \label{eq:braking}
\end{equation}
where $a_{\max} = 8.0$\,m/s$^2$.
This controller is trivially verifiable: the ego vehicle is guaranteed to stop within $d_{\text{stop}}$ meters.

\begin{figure*}[!t]
    \centering
    \input{figures/system_architecture.tex}
    \caption{The \textsc{Simplex-Track} architecture. The Advanced Channel (perception pipeline: YOLOv9 $\to$ DeepSORT $\to$ SF-EKF) provides state estimates. The Decision Module evaluates TTC-based STL robustness. Upon detecting $\rho(\varphi) < 0$, control transfers to the verified Emergency Braking baseline. ODD and uncertainty monitors provide additional safety margins.}
    \label{fig:system_arch}
\end{figure*}

The formal safety guarantee, following~\cite{sha2001simplex}, is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{TTC}_k^{(i)} \geq \tau_{\text{safe}} \implies d_k^{(i)} \geq d_{\text{stop}} + d_{\text{margin}}
\end{equation}
That is, if the monitor triggers at $\tau_{\text{safe}}$, the baseline has sufficient distance to stop before collision, provided the TTC estimate is accurate.

\subsection{Failure Distribution Analysis}

The MCMC analysis (Section~\ref{sec:mcmc}) reveals three distinct failure modes:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Close-range broadside} ($d < 14$\,m, $\theta \in [55^\circ, 110^\circ]$): The dominant failure mode (68\% of failures). The pedestrian appears suddenly in the ego's path with insufficient time for the EKF to converge.
    \item \textbf{Occluded emergence} ($d < 10$\,m, any $\theta$): The pedestrian is initially occluded by another agent or object, causing late detection by YOLOv9 (22\% of failures).
    \item \textbf{Group dynamics} ($N_{\text{ped}} \geq 7$, $d \in [10, 20]$\,m): Dense pedestrian groups create ambiguous social force interactions, degrading EKF predictions (10\% of failures).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Key Insights}

\paragraph{Social Force helps most at medium density}
The SF model improves prediction when pedestrians actively avoid each other, but this benefit saturates in dense crowds where group dynamics dominate pairwise interactions.

\paragraph{Importance sampling dramatically reduces sample requirements}
The 5.2$\times$ variance reduction from IS means that equivalent estimation precision can be achieved with $\sim$96 samples instead of $\sim$500, translating to a $5\times$ reduction in expensive CARLA simulation time.

\paragraph{The Simplex monitor cannot compensate for detection failures}
When YOLOv9 misses a pedestrian entirely (no track is created), the TTC monitor has no information to act on.
This highlights the fundamental limitation of runtime monitoring: it can only intervene on \emph{detected} hazards.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Simulation-to-real gap:} All validation is conducted in CARLA. Real-world sensor noise, weather variation, and pedestrian behavior may differ substantially.
    \item \textbf{Ground-truth detections:} While YOLOv9 is used for detection, our failure analysis conflates detection errors with tracking errors. Isolating the tracker requires ground-truth detections.
    \item \textbf{Fixed ego policy:} The ego vehicle follows a predetermined route at constant speed. An adaptive ego planner would alter the failure distribution.
    \item \textbf{Pairwise social force:} The Helbing--Moln\'{a}r model uses pairwise interactions. Group-level or learning-based models~\cite{alahi2016social} may better capture dense crowd dynamics.
    \item \textbf{TTC accuracy:} The Simplex monitor's guarantee depends on accurate TTC computation. State estimation errors propagate into TTC, potentially causing both missed detections and false alarms.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \textsc{Simplex-Track}, a systematic validation framework for behavior-aware pedestrian tracking, applying the full safety validation pipeline from Kochenderfer et al.~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation} to a Social Force EKF tracker in CARLA.

Our analysis reveals that the SF-EKF reduces collision rates by 52\% over a constant-velocity baseline, with the largest improvements at medium pedestrian density where social interactions are most predictive.
MCMC sampling identifies a concentrated failure region (close-range, broadside approaches) that accounts for 68\% of collisions.
Importance sampling provides a 5.2$\times$ variance reduction for failure probability estimation, enabling precise characterization with limited simulation budget.
The Simplex runtime monitor further reduces collisions by 73\% at the cost of a 6.3\% false positive brake rate.

These results demonstrate that formal validation methods---STL specification, optimization-based falsification, MCMC failure characterization, importance sampling, and runtime monitoring---can be effectively applied to perception-level components, not just control systems.
The identified failure modes and probability estimates provide actionable guidance for system improvement: addressing close-range detection latency and dense-crowd social force modeling would yield the greatest safety gains.
