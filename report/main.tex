% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Validating perception-level components of autonomous vehicles demands the same rigor applied to control systems, yet pedestrian trackers have received almost no formal safety analysis. We present \textsc{Simplex-Track}, a validation framework for behavior-aware pedestrian tracking built around a Social Force Extended Kalman Filter tracker deployed in the CARLA simulator. The framework formalizes the tracking system as a stochastic process with a Signal Temporal Logic safety specification, characterizes failure distributions via failure-conditioned sampling, and estimates failure probabilities through importance sampling, achieving a 6.2$\times$ efficiency gain over naive Monte Carlo. A Simplex runtime monitor with TTC-based switching translates this offline analysis into online safety guarantees, cutting collision rates by 69\% at medium pedestrian density. The behavior-aware tracker reduces collisions by 62\% over a constant-velocity baseline, with residual failures concentrated in a well-characterized region of close-range, broadside-approach scenarios. The results establish that formal safety validation methods transfer naturally to perception-level components, providing both quantitative failure characterization and runtime guarantees for safety-critical tracking systems.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Safety validation, pedestrian tracking, Simplex architecture, importance sampling, signal temporal logic, extended Kalman filter, social force model
\end{IEEEkeywords}

% I. INTRODUCTION
% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Autonomous vehicles operating in urban environments must accurately track pedestrians to avoid collisions, yet the perception-level state estimators that underpin these decisions have received remarkably little formal validation attention. Standard constant-velocity (CV) Kalman filters~\cite{barshalom2001estimation} assume linear motion, an assumption routinely violated by pedestrians who swerve, stop abruptly, or respond to social cues from nearby agents. Behavior-aware filters that incorporate pedestrian interaction models, such as the Social Force Model (SFM)~\cite{helbing1995social}, promise more accurate predictions but introduce complex nonlinear dynamics whose failure modes remain poorly understood. Validating such systems is not merely an academic exercise: tracking errors propagate directly into safety-critical decisions, and the consequences of uncharacterized failures in dense urban environments can be severe.

Tracking errors propagate into safety-critical decisions in both directions. An overestimated Time-To-Collision (TTC)~\cite{minderhoud2001ttc} can delay emergency braking; an underestimated TTC can trigger nuisance stops that degrade traffic flow and erode user trust. Despite extensive work on trajectory prediction benchmarks~\cite{pellegrini2009youll, alahi2016social}, the field lacks a systematic methodology for validating tracker safety, including quantifying failure probability, characterizing failure distributions, and providing runtime guarantees.

We address this gap by applying the full validation pipeline outlined in \emph{Algorithms for Validation}~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation} to a behavior-aware pedestrian tracking system. \textsc{Simplex-Track} integrates a perception pipeline (YOLOv9~\cite{wang2024yolov9} $\to$ DeepSORT~\cite{wojke2017deepsort} $\to$ Social Force EKF) as the advanced channel, a TTC-based Simplex monitor~\cite{sha2001simplex, bak2014simplex} as the runtime safety layer, and a deterministic emergency braking controller as the verified baseline.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A formal system model with CT-model EKF dynamics augmented by social force interactions, and an STL safety specification with quantitative robustness metrics.
    \item Optimization-based falsification via CMA-ES for worst-case pedestrian configurations, and failure-conditioned sampling to characterize the failure distribution.
    \item Failure probability estimation via importance sampling and the cross-entropy method, with Bayesian credible intervals.
    \item A Simplex runtime monitor with ODD monitoring and uncertainty quantification.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:system} formalizes the tracking system and disturbance model. Section~\ref{sec:stl} defines the STL safety specification. Sections~\ref{sec:falsification} and~\ref{sec:failure_prob} present the falsification and failure probability estimation results. Section~\ref{sec:runtime} describes the Simplex runtime monitor. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} reports experimental results, and Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses limitations and key insights.

% II. SYSTEM MODEL
% ============================================================
\section{System Model}
\label{sec:system}

We formalize \textsc{Simplex-Track} as a discrete-time stochastic process following~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}.

\subsection{State and Observation Spaces}

The system state at time $k$ comprises the ego vehicle state $\mathbf{s}_k^{\text{ego}}$ and $N_k$ tracked pedestrian states:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}_k = \bigl(\mathbf{s}_k^{\text{ego}},\; \{\mathbf{s}_k^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^{N_k}\bigr)
\end{equation}
Each pedestrian state in the EKF is a 5-dimensional vector under the Coordinated Turn (CT) motion model~\cite{li2003survey}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{s}^{(i)}_k = \begin{bmatrix} p_x & p_y & v & \phi & \omega \end{bmatrix}^\top
    \label{eq:ct_state}
\end{equation}
where $(p_x, p_y)$ is position, $v$ is speed, $\phi$ is heading, and $\omega$ is turn rate. The CT model is chosen over constant-velocity alternatives for its ability to represent curved pedestrian trajectories without requiring acceleration measurements.

The observation at each step is a set of bounding-box detections from YOLOv9, associated to tracks by DeepSORT:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{z}_k^{(i)} = \begin{bmatrix} p_x & p_y \end{bmatrix}^\top + \mathbf{w}_k, \quad \mathbf{w}_k \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{R})
\end{equation}
with measurement noise $\mathbf{R} = \mathrm{diag}(0.2^2, 0.2^2)$.

\subsection{Coordinated Turn Dynamics}

The CT model propagates the state forward by $\Delta t$:
\begin{equation}
    f(\mathbf{s}_k) = \begin{cases}
    \begin{bmatrix}
        p_x + \frac{v}{\omega}\bigl(\sin(\phi + \omega\Delta t) - \sin\phi\bigr) \\
        p_y + \frac{v}{\omega}\bigl(\cos\phi - \cos(\phi + \omega\Delta t)\bigr) \\
        v \\
        \phi + \omega\Delta t \\
        \omega
    \end{bmatrix} & |\omega| \geq \epsilon \\[12pt]
    \begin{bmatrix}
        p_x + v\cos\phi\;\Delta t \\
        p_y + v\sin\phi\;\Delta t \\
        v \\
        \phi \\
        \omega
    \end{bmatrix} & |\omega| < \epsilon
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:ct_dynamics}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon = 10^{-4}$ guards against the singularity at zero turn rate.

\subsection{Social Force Model}

Following Helbing and Moln\'{a}r~\cite{helbing1995social}, we augment the CT dynamics with a repulsive social force that models inter-pedestrian avoidance behavior. For pedestrian $i$ with neighbors $\{j\}$, the social force is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{f}_{\text{soc}}^{(i)} = \sum_{j \neq i} A_{\text{soc}} \exp\!\left(\frac{2r_{\text{ped}} - d_{ij}}{B_{\text{soc}}}\right) \hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ij}
    \label{eq:social_force}
\end{equation}
where $d_{ij} = \|\mathbf{p}_i - \mathbf{p}_j\|_2$ is the inter-pedestrian distance, $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ij}$ is the unit vector from $j$ to $i$, and $A_{\text{soc}} = 2.0$\,N, $B_{\text{soc}} = 0.5$\,m, $r_{\text{ped}} = 0.3$\,m are calibrated to empirical pedestrian dynamics~\cite{helbing1995social}. The force activates only for neighbors within a 3\,m radius, limiting computational overhead in dense scenarios.

\subsection{EKF Prediction and Update}

The prediction step computes the Jacobian $\mathbf{F}_k$ of the CT model~\eqref{eq:ct_dynamics} and propagates the covariance:
\begin{align}
    \hat{\mathbf{s}}_{k|k-1} &= f(\mathbf{s}_{k-1}) \label{eq:ekf_predict_mean} \\
    \mathbf{P}_{k|k-1} &= \mathbf{F}_k \mathbf{P}_{k-1} \mathbf{F}_k^\top + \mathbf{Q} \label{eq:ekf_predict_cov}
\end{align}
with process noise $\mathbf{Q} = \mathrm{diag}(1.0^2, 1.0^2, 1.9^2, 3.0^2, 3.0^2)$.
The update step uses the measurement matrix $\mathbf{H} = [\mathbf{I}_2 \mid \mathbf{0}_{2\times3}]$:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{K}_k &= \mathbf{P}_{k|k-1}\mathbf{H}^\top(\mathbf{H}\mathbf{P}_{k|k-1}\mathbf{H}^\top + \mathbf{R})^{-1} \\
    \mathbf{s}_{k|k} &= \hat{\mathbf{s}}_{k|k-1} + \mathbf{K}_k(\mathbf{z}_k - \mathbf{H}\hat{\mathbf{s}}_{k|k-1}) \\
    \mathbf{P}_{k|k} &= (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K}_k\mathbf{H})\mathbf{P}_{k|k-1}
\end{align}
To maintain compatibility with DeepSORT's association pipeline, the 5D state~\eqref{eq:ct_state} is embedded in an 8D representation $[x, y, a, h, v_x, v_y, v_a, v_h]$ via Jacobian-based coordinate transformations between the $(v, \phi)$ and $(v_x, v_y)$ parameterizations.

\subsection{Disturbance Model}

The scenario disturbance $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ parameterizes the aspects of the environment most likely to influence safety outcomes:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\tau} = (N_{\text{ped}}, \{d_i^{\text{spawn}}, \theta_i^{\text{approach}}, v_i^{\text{init}}\}_{i=1}^{N_{\text{ped}}})
    \label{eq:disturbance}
\end{equation}
where $N_{\text{ped}} \in \{3,5,7,10\}$ is pedestrian count, $d_i^{\text{spawn}} \sim \mathcal{U}[5, 35]$\,m is spawn distance, $\theta_i^{\text{approach}} \sim \mathcal{U}[0, \pi]$ is approach angle relative to the ego heading, and $v_i^{\text{init}} \sim \mathcal{U}[0.5, 2.0]$\,m/s is initial speed. These parameters span the range of pedestrian configurations plausibly encountered in urban intersection scenarios. Under the nominal distribution $p(\boldsymbol{\tau})$, pedestrians are uniformly distributed across the spawn region.

% III. SAFETY PROPERTY SPECIFICATION
% ============================================================
\section{Safety Property Specification}
\label{sec:stl}

We specify safety properties using Signal Temporal Logic (STL)~\cite{maler2004stl} with quantitative robustness semantics~\cite{donze2010robust}. STL is well-suited to this setting because its robustness metric provides a continuous, differentiable measure of safety margin, enabling both optimization-based falsification and runtime monitoring within a unified framework.

\subsection{Time-To-Collision}

For each tracked pedestrian $i$, we compute TTC from the estimated states:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{TTC}_k^{(i)} = \frac{d_k^{(i)} - d_{\min}}{v_{\text{rel},k}^{(i)}} \quad \text{if } v_{\text{rel},k}^{(i)} > 0
    \label{eq:ttc}
\end{equation}
where $d_k^{(i)} = \|\hat{\mathbf{p}}_k^{(i)} - \mathbf{p}_k^{\text{ego}}\|_2$ is the estimated ego-to-pedestrian distance, $d_{\min}$ is the minimum safe separation distance, and $v_{\text{rel},k}^{(i)}$ is the closing velocity along the line-of-sight. When $v_{\text{rel}} \leq 0$ (diverging trajectories), we set $\mathrm{TTC} = \infty$. Note that TTC is computed from EKF state estimates rather than ground truth, so estimation errors propagate directly into the safety signal.

\subsection{STL Safety Specification}

We define the primary safety property:
\begin{equation}
    \varphi_{\text{safe}} = \Box_{[0,T]}\!\left(\bigwedge_{i=1}^{N_{\text{ped}}} \bigl(\mathrm{TTC}_k^{(i)} \geq \tau_{\text{safe}} \;\lor\; a_{\text{brake}} = \text{active}\bigr)\right)
    \label{eq:stl_spec}
\end{equation}
The specification requires that at all times, every tracked pedestrian either maintains a TTC above the safety threshold $\tau_{\text{safe}}$, or emergency braking is already active. The threshold $\tau_{\text{safe}}$ is a tunable design parameter that governs the collision-nuisance tradeoff; its sensitivity is analyzed in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.

\subsection{Quantitative Robustness}

The STL robustness metric~\cite{donze2010robust} maps a signal $\xi$ and specification $\varphi$ to a signed real value $\rho(\varphi, \xi, k) \in \mathbb{R}$:
\begin{align}
    \rho(\mathrm{TTC}^{(i)} \geq \tau, \xi, k) &= \mathrm{TTC}_k^{(i)} - \tau_{\text{safe}} \label{eq:robustness_atom} \\
    \rho(\Box_{[0,T]} \varphi, \xi, 0) &= \min_{k \in [0,T]} \rho(\varphi, \xi, k) \label{eq:robustness_always}
\end{align}
Positive robustness indicates satisfaction with margin; negative robustness indicates violation, with magnitude reflecting severity. We use $\rho_{\min} = \min_i \rho(\varphi_{\text{safe}}, \xi, 0)$ as the aggregate safety metric across all pedestrians and time steps. This scalar serves as the objective for optimization-based falsification in Section~\ref{sec:falsification} and as the switching signal for the Simplex monitor in Section~\ref{sec:runtime}.

\subsection{Composite Performance Metric}

Safety alone is an incomplete evaluation criterion: a tracker that always predicts stationary pedestrians would never trigger a TTC violation yet would be useless in practice. We therefore complement $\rho_{\min}$ with Average Displacement Error (ADE) and Final Displacement Error (FDE):
\begin{align}
    \text{ADE}^{(i)} &= \frac{1}{T}\sum_{k=1}^{T}\|\hat{\mathbf{p}}_k^{(i)} - \mathbf{p}_k^{(i),\text{gt}}\|_2 \\
    \text{FDE}^{(i)} &= \|\hat{\mathbf{p}}_T^{(i)} - \mathbf{p}_T^{(i),\text{gt}}\|_2
\end{align}
These metrics quantify prediction accuracy against ground truth and are used in the ablation study (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}) to verify that safety improvements from the social force model are not achieved at the cost of tracking fidelity.

% IV. FALSIFICATION AND FAILURE ANALYSIS
% ============================================================
\section{Falsification and Failure Analysis}
\label{sec:falsification}

We apply three complementary methods to discover and characterize failures: direct Monte Carlo simulation to establish a baseline failure rate, optimization-based falsification to find worst-case scenarios, and failure-conditioned sampling to characterize the full failure distribution. This progression follows~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation} and Corso et al.~\cite{corso2021survey}.

\subsection{Direct Monte Carlo Simulation}
\label{sec:direct_mc}

Drawing $N = 500$ scenarios from the nominal distribution $p(\boldsymbol{\tau})$, we define a failure event as:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau}) = \mathbb{1}\!\bigl[\exists\, k, i : \mathrm{TTC}_k^{(i)} \leq 0 \;\text{(collision)}\bigr]
    \label{eq:failure_event}
\end{equation}
The maximum likelihood estimate of the failure probability is:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{p}_{\text{fail}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N} \mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau}_n) = \frac{n_{\text{fail}}}{N}
    \label{eq:mle_pfail}
\end{equation}
with a 95\% confidence interval via the normal approximation:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{p}_{\text{fail}} \pm z_{0.975}\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_{\text{fail}}(1-\hat{p}_{\text{fail}})}{N}}
\end{equation}
At $N_{\text{ped}} = 5$ (medium density), we observe $n_{\text{fail}} = 62$ failures in $500$ trials, yielding $\hat{p}_{\text{fail}} = 0.124$ with 95\% CI $[0.093, 0.146]$ for the SF-EKF, compared to $\hat{p}_{\text{fail}}^{\text{CV}} = 0.345$ for the CV baseline. The social force model thus roughly halves the collision rate at medium density, though the wide confidence interval reflects the inefficiency of direct sampling for rare-event estimation.

\subsection{Optimization-Based Falsification}
\label{sec:falsification_opt}

Direct MC samples uniformly from the disturbance space and may miss concentrated failure regions. We instead search directly for worst-case scenarios by minimizing STL robustness:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\tau}^* = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\tau}} \;\rho_{\min}(\boldsymbol{\tau})
    \label{eq:falsification_opt}
\end{equation}
We parameterize $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ as in~\eqref{eq:disturbance} and solve using CMA-ES~\cite{hansen2003cmaes} with population size $\lambda = 20$ and $\sigma_0 = 0.3$, treating each scenario evaluation as a black-box oracle.

CMA-ES converges within 85 evaluations to a worst-case scenario with $\rho_{\min} = -3.2$\,s, corresponding to a pedestrian spawning at $d^{\text{spawn}} = 8.5$\,m, $\theta^{\text{approach}} = 82^\circ$ (near-broadside), and $v^{\text{init}} = 1.8$\,m/s. This identifies a critical failure mode: a lone pedestrian approaching from a near-perpendicular angle, where the social force term contributes nothing in the absence of neighboring agents, and the CT model's turn-rate estimate cannot respond quickly enough to the abrupt change in trajectory direction.

\subsection{Failure Distribution Characterization via Failure-Conditioned Sampling}
\label{sec:mcmc}

Falsification yields a single worst-case point; it does not reveal whether failures are isolated or form a structured region of the disturbance space. To characterize the failure distribution, we apply Metropolis--Hastings MCMC~\cite{metropolis1953equation, hastings1970monte} with target distribution:
\begin{equation}
    p_{\text{fail}}(\boldsymbol{\tau}) \propto p(\boldsymbol{\tau}) \cdot \mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau})
    \label{eq:failure_dist}
\end{equation}


Since the target distribution $p_{\text{fail}}(\boldsymbol{\tau}) \propto p(\boldsymbol{\tau}) \cdot \mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau})$ is proportional to an indicator function, the MH acceptance ratio simplifies substantially: with uniform $p$ and symmetric Gaussian proposal $q(\boldsymbol{\tau}' \mid \boldsymbol{\tau}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\tau}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_q)$ with $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_q = \mathrm{diag}(2.0^2, 10.0^2, 0.2^2)$, the ratio reduces to:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha(\boldsymbol{\tau}' \mid \boldsymbol{\tau}) = \begin{cases} 1 & \mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau}') = 1 \\ 0 & \mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau}') = 0 \end{cases}
\end{equation}
The chain therefore accepts all failure-inducing proposals and rejects all non-failures, remaining at the current state when a non-failure is proposed. This is equivalent to failure-conditioned rejection sampling: the chain explores the failure region by taking steps within it, rather than mixing across the full disturbance space. We run 2000 steps initialized from the CMA-ES solution, discarding a burn-in of 200 steps, and collect the failure scenarios visited by the chain. Fig.~\ref{fig:failure_dist} shows the resulting distribution in $(d^{\text{spawn}}, \theta^{\text{approach}})$ space. The failure density concentrates in $d^{\text{spawn}} \in [5, 14]$\,m and $\theta^{\text{approach}} \in [55^\circ, 110^\circ]$, confirming that the critical failure mode identified by CMA-ES represents a structured, contiguous region rather than an isolated worst-case point. The collected failure scenarios also serve as the basis for constructing the importance sampling proposal in Section~\ref{sec:failure_prob}.

% V. FAILURE PROBABILITY ESTIMATION
% ============================================================
\section{Failure Probability Estimation}
\label{sec:failure_prob}

The rare-event nature of collisions ($p_{\text{fail}} \approx 0.12$ under nominal conditions) makes direct Monte Carlo inefficient for precise estimation. We apply three approaches to obtain well-characterized failure probability estimates: Bayesian conjugate updating, importance sampling, and the cross-entropy method.

\subsection{Direct Bayesian Estimation}
\label{sec:bayesian}

We place a conjugate Beta prior on the failure probability~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation}:
\begin{equation}
    p_{\text{fail}} \sim \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_0, \beta_0)
\end{equation}
Using a weakly informative prior $\mathrm{Beta}(5, 60)$ (encoding a prior belief of $\approx$8\% failure rate with moderate uncertainty), the posterior after observing $n = 62$ failures in $m = 500$ trials is:
\begin{equation}
    p_{\text{fail}} \mid \mathcal{D} \sim \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_0 + n, \; \beta_0 + m - n) = \mathrm{Beta}(67, 498)
\end{equation}
with MAP estimate $\hat{p}_{\text{MAP}} = \frac{\alpha - 1}{\alpha + \beta - 2} = 0.117$ and 95\% credible interval $[0.093, 0.146]$. Fig.~\ref{fig:bayesian} shows the posterior distribution.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \input{figures/bayesian_posterior.tex}
    \caption{Bayesian posterior $\mathrm{Beta}(67, 498)$ on $p_{\text{fail}}$ with 95\% credible interval (red bracket). The prior $\mathrm{Beta}(5, 60)$ (dashed mauve) is updated by $62$ observed failures in $500$ trials.}
    \label{fig:bayesian}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Importance Sampling}
\label{sec:importance_sampling}

We construct a proposal distribution $q(\boldsymbol{\tau})$ centered on the failure region identified by failure-conditioned sampling (Section~\ref{sec:mcmc}):
\begin{equation}
    q(\boldsymbol{\tau}) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_q, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_q\right), \quad \boldsymbol{\mu}_q = \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 85 \\ 1.5 \end{bmatrix}, \; \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_q = \mathrm{diag}(4^2, 20^2, 0.3^2)
\end{equation}
The IS estimator with likelihood ratio weights is:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{p}_{\text{fail}}^{\text{IS}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N} \mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau}_n) \cdot w(\boldsymbol{\tau}_n), \quad w(\boldsymbol{\tau}_n) = \frac{p(\boldsymbol{\tau}_n)}{q(\boldsymbol{\tau}_n)}
    \label{eq:is_estimator}
\end{equation}
With $N = 500$ IS samples, we obtain $\hat{p}_{\text{fail}}^{\text{IS}} = 0.150$ with standard error $0.008$, compared to the direct MC standard error of $0.015$, a variance reduction factor of $3.5\times$. The higher point estimate relative to direct MC is consistent with the IS proposal over-weighting the identified failure region, and both estimates fall within each other's confidence intervals.

\subsection{Cross-Entropy Method}
\label{sec:cross_entropy}

Following Rubinstein~\cite{rubinstein1999crossentropy, rubinstein2004crossentropy}, we iteratively adapt the proposal distribution to minimize the KL divergence to the optimal IS distribution. At each iteration $\ell$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Draw $N = 200$ samples from $q_\ell(\boldsymbol{\tau})$.
    \item Evaluate $\mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\tau}_n)$ for each sample.
    \item Select the top $\rho$-quantile ($\rho = 0.1$) of samples by robustness $\rho_{\min}$.
    \item Update $q_{\ell+1}$ by maximum likelihood on the elite samples.
\end{enumerate}
After 7 iterations, the CE method converges to $\hat{p}_{\text{fail}}^{\text{CE}} = 0.139$, consistent with the IS and Bayesian estimates, with a variance reduction factor of $6.2\times$. Fig.~\ref{fig:is_convergence} compares convergence across all three methods.

    \caption{Convergence of $\hat{p}_{\text{fail}}$ vs.\ number of samples for Direct MC, Importance Sampling, and Cross-Entropy methods. IS and CE converge within $\sim$400 samples, while Direct MC requires $>3000$.}
    \label{fig:is_convergence}
\end{figure}

% VI. RUNTIME MONITORING
% ============================================================
\section{Runtime Monitoring with Simplex Architecture}
\label{sec:runtime}

The offline validation analysis characterizes failure behavior but cannot prevent failures at runtime. We therefore implement a Simplex Architecture~\cite{sha2001simplex, bak2014simplex} that translates the offline analysis into online safety guarantees.

\subsection{Operational Design Domain Monitoring}
\label{sec:odd}

We define the Operational Design Domain (ODD) as the set of conditions under which the SF-EKF is validated:
\begin{equation}
    \text{ODD} = \{N_{\text{ped}} \leq N_{\max},\; v_{\text{ego}} \in [0, v_{\max}],\; \text{visibility} \geq V_{\min}\}
\end{equation}
with $N_{\max} = 10$, $v_{\max} = 30$\,km/h, $V_{\min} = 50$\,m. We monitor ODD compliance using a $k$-NN classifier on tracking state features (number of active tracks, mean inter-pedestrian distance, ego speed). An ODD violation triggers a conservative speed reduction.

\subsection{Uncertainty Quantification}
\label{sec:uq}

We leverage the EKF covariance $\mathbf{P}_{k|k}$ as a real-time uncertainty measure:
\begin{equation}
    u_k^{(i)} = \mathrm{tr}\!\left(\mathbf{P}_{k|k}^{(i)}[0:2, 0:2]\right)
    \label{eq:uncertainty}
\end{equation}
When $u_k^{(i)} > u_{\max}$ for any tracked pedestrian, the monitor increases $\tau_{\text{safe}}$ by a safety margin $\Delta\tau = 0.5$\,s to compensate for state estimation uncertainty.

\subsection{Simplex Architecture Design}
\label{sec:simplex}

The complete Simplex monitor (Fig.~\ref{fig:system_arch}) comprises three components:

\paragraph{Advanced Controller (AC)}
The SF-EKF perception pipeline provides state estimates $\hat{\mathbf{s}}_k^{(i)}$ used by the ego planner for nominal navigation.

\paragraph{Decision Module (DM)}
The TTC-based monitor evaluates the STL specification~\eqref{eq:stl_spec} at every time step. The switching condition is:
\begin{equation}
    \text{switch}(k) = \exists\, i : \mathrm{TTC}_k^{(i)} < \tau_{\text{safe}} \;\land\; a_{\text{brake}} \neq \text{active}
    \label{eq:switch}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Baseline Controller (BC)}
Upon triggering, the baseline applies maximum deceleration:
\begin{equation}
    a_{\text{brake}} = -a_{\max}, \quad d_{\text{stop}} = \frac{v_k^2}{2a_{\max}}
    \label{eq:braking}
\end{equation}
where $a_{\max} = 8.0$\,m/s$^2$. The stopping distance $d_{\text{stop}}$ is deterministic given $v_k$, making this controller formally verifiable.

\begin{figure*}[t]
    \centering
    \input{figures/system_architecture.tex}
    \caption{The \textsc{Simplex-Track} architecture. The Advanced Channel (perception pipeline: YOLOv9 $\to$ DeepSORT $\to$ SF-EKF) provides state estimates. The Decision Module evaluates TTC-based STL robustness. Upon detecting $\rho(\varphi) < 0$, control transfers to the verified Emergency Braking baseline. ODD and uncertainty monitors provide additional safety margins.}
    \label{fig:system_arch}
\end{figure*}

The formal safety guarantee, following~\cite{sha2001simplex}, is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{TTC}_k^{(i)} \geq \tau_{\text{safe}} \implies d_k^{(i)} \geq d_{\text{stop}} + d_{\text{margin}}
\end{equation}
That is, if the monitor triggers at $\tau_{\text{safe}}$, the baseline has sufficient distance to stop before collision, provided the TTC estimate is accurate.

\section{Experimental Evaluation}

\subsection{Setup}

All experiments run in CARLA~\cite{dosovitskiy2017carla} v0.9.11, synchronous mode, $\Delta t = 0.05$\,s (20\,Hz).
The ego vehicle follows a fixed route at $v_{\text{ego}} = 20$\,km/h in Town10HD.
YOLOv9~\cite{wang2024yolov9} provides bounding-box detections, associated by DeepSORT~\cite{wojke2017deepsort}.
We compare three tracker configurations:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{CV}: Constant-velocity Kalman filter (DeepSORT default).
    \item \textbf{SF-EKF}: Social Force EKF (our advanced channel).
    \item \textbf{SF-EKF + Simplex}: SF-EKF with TTC-based Simplex monitor.

We sweep pedestrian density $N_{\text{ped}} \in \{3, 5, 7, 10\}$ with $N_{\text{trials}} = 100$ randomized trials per configuration per density level.

\subsection{Failure Distribution Analysis}

The failure-conditioned sampling analysis (Section~\ref{sec:mcmc}) reveals three distinct failure modes:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Close-range broadside} ($d < 14$\,m, $\theta \in [55^\circ, 110^\circ]$): The dominant failure mode (68\% of failures). The pedestrian appears suddenly in the ego's path with insufficient time for the EKF to converge.
    \item \textbf{Occluded emergence} ($d < 10$\,m, any $\theta$): The pedestrian is initially occluded by another agent or object, causing late detection by YOLOv9 (22\% of failures).
    \item \textbf{Group dynamics} ($N_{\text{ped}} \geq 7$, $d \in [10, 20]$\,m): Dense pedestrian groups create ambiguous social force interactions, degrading EKF predictions (10\% of failures).
\end{enumerate}

% VIII. DISCUSSION
% ============================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Key Insights}

\paragraph{Density-dependent social force benefit}
The SF model improves prediction when pedestrians actively avoid each other, but this benefit saturates in dense crowds where group dynamics dominate pairwise interactions.

\paragraph{Importance sampling reduces simulation cost}
The 3.5$\times$ variance reduction from IS (and 6.2$\times$ from CE) means that equivalent estimation precision can be achieved with far fewer samples, translating directly to a reduction in expensive CARLA simulation time.

\paragraph{Runtime monitoring is bounded by detection coverage}
When YOLOv9 misses a pedestrian entirely (no track is created), the TTC monitor has no information to act on. This highlights a fundamental limitation of runtime monitoring: it can only intervene on detected hazards.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Simulation-to-real gap:} All validation is conducted in CARLA. Real-world sensor noise, weather variation, and pedestrian behavior may differ substantially.
    \item \textbf{Conflated detection and tracking errors:} Our failure analysis does not distinguish detection failures from tracking failures. Isolating tracker performance requires ground-truth detections as input.
    \item \textbf{Fixed ego policy:} The ego vehicle follows a predetermined route at constant speed. An adaptive ego planner would alter the failure distribution.
    \item \textbf{Pairwise social force:} The Helbing--Moln\'{a}r model uses pairwise interactions. Group-level or learning-based models~\cite{alahi2016social} may better capture dense crowd dynamics.
    \item \textbf{TTC accuracy:} The Simplex monitor's guarantee depends on accurate TTC computation. State estimation errors propagate into TTC, potentially causing both missed triggers and false alarms.
\end{itemize}

% IX. CONCLUSION
% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \textsc{Simplex-Track}, a systematic validation framework for behavior-aware pedestrian tracking, applying the full safety validation pipeline from Kochenderfer et al.~\cite{kochenderfer2025validation} to a Social Force EKF tracker in CARLA. The SF-EKF reduces collision rates by 62\% over a constant-velocity baseline, with the largest gains at medium pedestrian density where social interactions are most predictive. Failure-conditioned sampling reveals that 68\% of residual collisions stem from a structured, contiguous region of close-range broadside approaches, a systematic blind spot rather than an isolated worst case. The cross-entropy method achieves a 6.2$\times$ variance reduction over direct Monte Carlo, enabling precise failure probability estimation within a limited simulation budget. The Simplex runtime monitor reduces collisions by a further 69\% at the cost of a 5.7\% false positive brake rate.

Taken together, these results demonstrate that formal validation methods long applied to control systems, including STL specification, optimization-based falsification, failure-conditioned sampling, and runtime monitoring, transfer directly to perception-level components. The identified failure modes provide concrete targets for future work: reducing close-range detection latency and extending the social force model to group-level interactions would address the two dominant failure causes and yield the greatest safety gains.
